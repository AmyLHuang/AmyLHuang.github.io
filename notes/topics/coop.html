Data Visualization Project & EDA Process

Apply knowledge of Excel and Tableau to complete a real-world data visualization project in teams, using the Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) framework.

EDA is the process of examining and summarizing a dataset to understand its main characteristics, patterns, and relationships before more advanced analysis. Steps include:

  • Ask questions
  • Collect and import data
  • Clean data
  • Explore data
  • Visualize data
  • Analyze and interpret
  • Share findings

Cover Letter

  1. Salutation: Professional greeting
  2. Intro: State role, objectives, source of job posting, and interests
  3. Body: 1–3 paragraphs referencing (not copying) your resume
  4. Making the Case: Provide evidence and examples of fit
  5. Closing: Thank reader, suggest next steps, request interview

Email Writing

Structure
  1. Subject line: Concise and specific
  2. Salutation: Correct spelling of name
  3. Body: Key point in first paragraph
  4. Signature: Relevant contact info (name, number, links)
  5. Spelling/Grammar: Double-check names, links, etc.

Example Intro: Dear [name], I hope your day is going well. I am [name] and I'm writing to/because X. [Explain how you got their contact.]

Effective Presentations

Do your research - preparation is key!

  • Setting: Adding context/framing around the topic for the educated but uniformed members of your audience
  • Characters: Who are your stakeholders? Who will be affected and how?
  • Problem: Simplify your problem/objective so your audience can comprehend
  • Events: This is your analysis! What did you find out during each step?
  • Conclusion: The result of your analysis tying all your storytelling together as well as your recommendation

Interview

How to tell a compelling story
  1. Setting the stage: When did it happen? What was the organization/college? What was your role?
  2. The beginning: What was the challenge or situation you were facing?
  3. The middle: What actions did you take? Why did you make those decisions? Be specific.
  4. The end: What were the results/outcomes?
  5. Reflection: What did you learn? How can this be applied to this and other roles?
Common interview questions:
  • Tell me about yourself.
  • Why are you interested in this position?
  • What are your strengths?
  • What are your weaknesses?
  • Give me an example of a time when you had to overcome a challenge at work.
  • Tell me about a time when you made a mistake at work.
  • Tell me about a time when you went above and beyond your job duties.
  • Why do you want to leave your current job?
  • Where do you see yourself in five years?
  • Do you have any questions for me?
Common questions to ask interviewer:
  • How would you describe the company's culture and leadership?
  • What kinds of projects would I be working on?
  • What is the typical career trajectory for someone in this role?
  • Why do you like working here?
  • What’s your favorite part about working here?
  • What does a typical day look like in this role?
  • What do new employees find surprising after they start?
  • Are there any training programs available to employees?
Post Inverview
  • Give thanks for the opportunity and confirm the expectations for next steps
  • Always send a follow up thank you note or email through your aliigned communication channel
  • Reiterate your interest in the role
  • When sending a follow up be sure to recall specific points from the interview that you appreciated or continue to think about
  • “Thank you so much for the opportunity to interview with you. I appreciate your time and I am very excited about the possibility of joining your team. I look forward to hearing from you soon.”
  • “I just wanted to check in and see if there was an update on the job opening. I’m still very interested and would love to know where things are at. Thanks!”
Professionalism and Industry Etiquette

Etiquette refers to the set of unspoken rules, norms, and behaviors expected and observed in society.

Code-switching: when someone changes how they speak, behave, or present themselves in different situations or circumstances

Business Communications

  • Observe and confirm: Work closely with your manager, confirm communication differences between clients, departments, and vendors
  • Double check your work: Before you hand over a file or document, check it throughly before it goes to review
  • Review and learn: Mistakes are ok, as long as we fix them, and don't repeat them
  • Who are you talking to? What shouldn't you say? It changes based on where you are, and who you are with
  • Be understood, no matter what your communication method is
  • It doesn't matter what you said, it matters what was heard
  • Be prepared to respond - "I don't know" in unremarkable. "I don't know, but I can ask/run a report" is noticeable.
